What are the implication of the concept or One-World Chemistry for chemistry education?


Two decades ago, Fourez22 asked the question:

                "Do we teach biology, chemistry, physics, mathematics, or do we teach young people how to cope with their own world?"


Answering this question has led science educators to a rethinking of scientific literacy and thinking about science education as in terms of ‘participation in the community’.23 This approach resonates well with the concept of One-World Chemistry that chemistry should embrace ‘being a science for the benefit of society’.


What are the implications for how chemistry is taught? One-World Chemistry does NOT argue for the abandonment of teaching individual sciences in favour of an undifferentiated ‘general science’.


It DOES argue for:

  • stressing the unity of scientific principles and thought processes from the earliest stages of science education;
  • the development of a clear and seep understanding of the principles and practice of chemistry as a science in its own right;
  • embedding in chemistry education, from a very early stage, a growing awareness of the ways that chemistry interconnects with other disciplines; the way that chemistry can be used to understand, explain and tackle problems in the physical and biological domains of world around us; and the way that chemistry and other disciplines can work together to develop a collective and comprehensive view of how physical and biological systems behave, interact and affect one another.

There is a need for chemistry educators to improve teaching at all levels to inspire the next generation of chemists as well as achieve a level of chemical literacy in the general population that is relevant to people’s lives. The approach will seek to lay the groundwork for thinking beyond the immediate problem to thinking about systems and using cross-disciplinary approaches

  • Chemistry must be taught through a problem-solving approach that encourages and tests understanding and deductive reasoning, rather than as a collection of facts that require rote learning.
  • Chemistry needs to be taught in context – not just the context of ‘applications’, but also relevance to society and contributing to meeting global challenges. This has implications for developing a broader chemistry literacy, since all chemistry knowledge can be applied for good or bad: it is people in every part of society, (including scientists, policy-makers and the public) who decide. Therefore, understanding the fundamentals and potentials of chemistry is required not only by chemistry practitioners but as part of the chemistry/scientific literacy of the general population, the media and policy makers.
  • There has been an increasing tendency in many countries for practical experimentation to be introduced later and/or the amount of time spent in practical work to be reduced, especially in response to economic downturns and the escalating costs of conducting experimental work safely. This tendency is extremely detrimental to the study of chemistry, which has always been based on the linkages between experimental observations and theoretical explanations; to the conduct of chemistry research, which requires that graduate students have acquired a good level of practical skill before they begin advanced work; to the preparation of graduates who are able to enter industrial and other applied employment; and to the creation of a broader chemistry literacy that encompasses familiarity with chemicals, observations and deductions.
  • At least in teaching the basic levels of chemistry, the use of microscale science24-26 equipment and approaches can assist not only in reducing the costs of chemicals, equipment and infrastructure required, but also reflects the principles of sustainability and ‘green chemistry’. Originally developed in Africa27 as a solution to the challenge of teaching chemistry with extremely limited resources, the microscience approach is an example of a ‘reverse innovation’28 that has spread around the world.29
