A Global action plan

A Global

  • OWC: What the chemical sciences can do
  • A Global action plan

If chemistry is to move from its current position to one which embraces the approaches advocated by One-World Chemistry, it will require considerable effort to overcome the well-recognised constraints of individual and institutional inertia and systemic barriers to change. Therefore, a Global Action Plan is necessary to achieve the key dimensions of chemistry reform required – the integration of chemistry as a field, reducing internal barriers between sub-divisions of the subject; the embracing of cross-disciplinary approaches in education and research; the adoption of systems thinking; the creation of a ‘one world’ orientation in which chemistry strives to be a science for the benefit of society; the adoption of a problem-solving mentality; the determined application of chemistry to ensuring a sustainable future for the planet; and an ethical approach in all aspects of the teaching, learning and practice of chemistry.

The elaboration of this Global Action Plan must involve the collective and collaborative effort of the entire chemical science community, including academia, industry, professional associations, academies and policy specialists. We do not seek to pre-empt the outcome of this effort, but offer a process and a framework for the full development of such a plan.

    • At the individual and institutional level: chemical scientists individually and in groups should reflect on the objectives of One-World Chemistry and the changes needed in structures and processes, in education, research and practice, to achieve these objectives. They should communicate their ideas through formal and informal publications and through the channels of the professional societies and bodies to which they belong.

    • At the national and international levels: relevant professional academic and industry associations should come together to debate the objectives and institute processes to reach consensus on the nature and content of a Global Action Plan for One-World Chemistry.

    • A mechanism for reaching consensus at the global level and for the subsequent promotion of the Global Action Plan and its implementation should be established. This might be led by an existing single agency or consortium of agencies (e.g. IUPAC, UNESCO); or, if such an approach would be assessed as likely to become bogged down in bureaucratic procedures and politics, it might be led by a newly formed international committee of commission, with voluntary support from existing international and national agencies.
